    Ionization front fluorescence

    Reionization is an era in cosmic history when the majority of cosmic matter transitioned from a neutral to an ionized state. It began when the first star-forming galaxies and quasars illuminated the previously-neutral cosmos with bubbles of hydrogen-ionizing radiation. It ends when these bubbles completely overlap and intergalactic hydrogen is almost completely ionized. But a detailed explanation of how and when reionization occured is still unknown. An improved understanding of reionization would better constrain how the first luminous sources formed and evolved which addresses a fundamental human questions: "How did we get here?".

    The aim of my research is to better constrain the timing of the tail-end of the reionization process. Ionization fronts (bubble edges) in some late-ending reionization models will glow as they carve away at the final neutral islands. Could ionization fronts glow brightly enough for current/near-future instruments could observe reionization in progress?

    Thermal History of Intergalactic Gas

    Radiation from bright, distant quasars travel through vast swathes of nearly empty space called the intergalactic medium (IGM). Neutral hydrogen in the intergalactic medium causes absorption features in the quasar spectrum known as the lyman alpha forest. Statistics of the lyman alpha forest have been successful for constraining the warmness/fuzziness of dark matter as well as the timing of reionization processes. But the standard statistic, the lyman alpha forest power spectrum, is unable to strongly constrain the IGM temperature-density relation, which is related to the IGM thermal history and the formation of galaxies. In this work, I explore the the utility of the lyman beta forest, which is sensitive to higher densities than the lyman alpha forest, to improve constraints on the IGM temperature-density relation.
